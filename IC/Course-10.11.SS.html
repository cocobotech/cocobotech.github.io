The 60 mV/dec Limit

Why can't we turn off a transistor instantly?
A deep dive into the thermodynamics of leakage current.


T = 300K  |  SS ≥ 60 mV/dec

1. The Physics: Energy Barriers

2. The Control: Capacitive Divider

3. The Complete Derivation

Definition of SS: The change in gate voltage required to increase drain current by one decade ($10\times$).

$$ SS = \frac{dV_{GS}}{d(\log_{10} I_D)} $$

1 Apply Chain Rule: Split into electrostatic term and transport term.

$$ SS = \underbrace{\frac{dV_{GS}}{d\psi_s}}_{\text{Capacitors}} \times \underbrace{\frac{d\psi_s}{d(\log_{10} I_D)}}_{\text{Boltzmann}} $$

2 Solve Transport Term:

From $I_D \propto e^{\frac{q\psi_s}{kT}}$, taking $\ln$: $\ln(I_D) = \frac{q}{kT}\psi_s$.
Convert to $\log_{10}$: $\ln(10) \cdot \log_{10}(I_D) = \frac{q}{kT}\psi_s$.
Derivative: $\frac{d\psi_s}{d(\log_{10}I_D)} = \ln(10) \frac{kT}{q}$.


3 Final Result:

$$ SS = \left( 1 + \frac{C_{dep} + C_{it}}{C_{ox}} \right) \underbrace{\ln(10) \frac{k_B T}{q}}_{\text{Thermodynamic Limit}} $$

The 60 mV/dec Limit

At room temperature ($T = 300 K$), the thermodynamic term becomes a fundamental constant.

  • k_B = 1.38e-23 J/K
  • T = 300 K
  • q = 1.602e-19 C
  • kT/q ≈ 25.85 mV
  • ln(10) ≈ 2.3026

Calculation:

2.3 × 26 mV ≈ 60 mV

Therefore, even if $C_{dep}=0$ and $C_{it}=0$,
SS cannot be less than 60 mV/dec.
